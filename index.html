I

Research Methods at a glance

The five techniques you must know — laboratory and field experiments, self-reports, observations, correlations, plus longitudinal designs.

Every research method has strengths and weaknesses, and a psychologist must evaluate how the method they have chosen contributes to the validity and reliability of their specific investigation. The 9990 syllabus expects you to describe the main features of each method, evaluate them in terms of reliability, validity and ethics, and apply your knowledge to a novel scenario.

Experiments

An experiment is an investigation looking for a causal relationship in which an independent variable (IV) is manipulated and is expected to be responsible for changes in the dependent variable (DV). At AS Level you only need to know two types: laboratory and field.

Laboratory — StrengthsLaboratory — Weaknesses
High control of variables, allowing cause-and-effect to be inferred with confidence.Artificial setting and tasks reduce ecological validity.
Standardised procedures and instructions make replication straightforward, supporting reliability.Features of the environment may reveal the aim, producing demand characteristics.
Field — StrengthsField — Weaknesses
High ecological validity — participants behave naturally in their everyday environment.Low control over extraneous and situational variables makes replication difficult.
Limited demand characteristics, since participants often do not know they are being studied.Ethical issues — participants typically cannot give informed consent or be debriefed.

Self-reports

Self-reports involve asking questions to gain information directly from participants. The two main types are questionnaires (mainly written) and interviews (verbal). Questions can be open (free response, qualitative) or closed (fixed responses, quantitative).

Questionnaire — StrengthsQuestionnaire — Weaknesses
A large sample can answer in a short time, increasing representativeness and generalisability.Closed questions may force answers that don't reflect the participant's true opinion.
Participants are more likely to give truthful answers without face-to-face pressure.Social desirability bias may still distort responses.
Interview — StrengthsInterview — Weaknesses
Open questions yield rich qualitative data revealing why participants behave or feel as they do.Face-to-face setting can lead to social desirability and reduced truthfulness.
Semi-structured and unstructured formats allow follow-up questions to clarify meaning.Interviewer bias can affect both questioning style and interpretation, lowering objectivity.

Observations

An observation involves watching human or animal behaviour. Observers can be overt/covert, participant/non-participant, in a naturalistic/controlled setting, and use structured/unstructured recording.

Observation — StrengthsObservation — Weaknesses
Naturalistic settings give high ecological validity — participants behave authentically.If participants know they're being observed (overt), they may show socially desirable behaviour.
Structured observations using behavioural checklists generate objective quantitative data.Naturalistic studies are difficult to replicate as variables are not controlled, lowering reliability.

Correlations

A statistical technique that measures the relationship between two co-variables. Neither variable is manipulated — both are measured. Correlations can be positive, negative, or absent, and vary in strength.

Correlation — StrengthsCorrelation — Weaknesses
Useful where manipulation would be unethical or impractical (e.g. stress and illness).Cannot establish cause and effect — a third variable may explain both.
Allows researchers to identify patterns and generate hypotheses for future experiments.Restricted to quantitative measured variables and cannot reveal the underlying reasons for behaviour.

Longitudinal studies

A longitudinal study collects data from the same participants on more than one occasion, often over months or years. It can use any research method (an experiment, questionnaire, observation, etc.) — what makes it longitudinal is the repeated testing.

Longitudinal — StrengthsLongitudinal — Weaknesses
Allows changes within participants to be tracked over time, removing participant variables.Time-consuming and expensive — can take years to complete.
Can establish long-term effects of an intervention or developmental change.Participant attrition (drop-out) reduces sample size and may bias findings.
Exam tip Examiners reward precise terminology. Don't write "the experimenter watched them" — write "a covert, non-participant, naturalistic, structured observation was conducted using a behavioural checklist." Each named feature can earn a mark.

III

Techniques & their required features

For the Paper 2 planning question, every technique has a fixed list of features you must cover. Memorise these — using features from the wrong technique will cost marks.

The 9990 syllabus (page 50) and the VinSchool rubric agree on the same set of features for each technique. To reach Level 5 (9–10 marks), all four features must be covered with appropriate detail, and the plan must include four logical procedural steps that make the study replicable.

Experiments — choose laboratory or field

  • Independent variable (IV) — the factor you manipulate, with at least two operationalised levels (e.g. "music played: pop vs. classical vs. silence").
  • Dependent variable (DV) — the factor you measure, fully operationalised (e.g. "number of words recalled out of 20 in 60 seconds").
  • Controls / standardisation — at least two specific controls (e.g. same room temperature, same instructions read aloud, same time limit).
  • Research design — independent measures, repeated measures, or matched pairs, with a justification linked to the brief.

Self-reports — choose questionnaire or interview

  • Open AND/OR closed questions — state which type(s) and why.
  • Examples of questions (4 items) — write four actual questions linked to the aim, not just topic areas.
  • Question scoring OR interpretations — explain how responses will be scored (e.g. 5-point Likert: 1=strongly disagree to 5=strongly agree) or interpreted (for open questions: thematic analysis).
  • Questionnaire only: paper-and-pencil OR online format.
    Interview only: structured / semi-structured / unstructured format.

Observational studies

  • Overt OR covert — are participants aware they are being observed?
  • Participant OR non-participant — is the observer joining the group, or watching from outside?
  • Naturalistic OR controlled — is it in the participants' natural environment, or a manipulated setting?
  • Structured OR unstructured — pre-set behavioural categories with event/time sampling, or free recording of all behaviours?

Correlational studies

  • Two co-variables — name them clearly; both are measured (neither is manipulated).
  • Operationalisation of co-variable 1 — the precise way it will be measured (e.g. "self-reported screen time per day in hours").
  • Operationalisation of co-variable 2 — the precise way it will be measured (e.g. "PHQ-9 depression score, range 0–27").
  • Description of graph and a directional hypothesis — predict positive or negative correlation, and describe what the scatter graph would look like.
Mark scheme reminder Bullet points are your friend on the 10-marker. They keep your writing legible and let you tag each feature visibly. The structure that gets full marks: state the feature → give detail of how it works → link it back to the brief (a real-world example using the names and aims given in the question).

Worked 10-marker examples

Two scenarios per technique — one easier, one harder — with full sample answers showing exactly how to hit Level 5.

Each example follows the same structure: scenario → bullet-point answer covering all four features → procedural steps for replicability. Tags below show which feature each bullet addresses (FEATURE), with detail (DETAIL) and brief link (BRIEF).

Technique 1 — Experiments

EasierCaffeine and reaction time (laboratory experiment)
Scenario. Tom is a sports scientist who wants to investigate whether caffeine improves reaction time. He has access to a sports lab with computerised reaction-time software. Plan a laboratory experiment to investigate this. Do not describe sample, sampling technique or ethical guidelines.
Sample answer (10 marks)
  • IV The independent variable will be the dose of caffeine consumed, with three levels: 0 mg (placebo), 100 mg, and 200 mg, given in identical-looking capsules DETAIL, so that Tom can see whether higher caffeine doses produce faster reactions BRIEF.
  • DV The dependent variable will be reaction time, operationalised as the mean response time in milliseconds across 20 trials of a computerised "press the spacebar when a red dot appears" task DETAIL, giving a precise reaction-time score for each athlete BRIEF.
  • CONTROLS Standardised controls will include: all participants tested in the same sports lab at the same time of day (10 a.m.), with identical computer monitors and standardised instructions read aloud from a script DETAIL; participants will also be asked to abstain from caffeine for 12 hours beforehand, ensuring any difference in reaction time is due to the caffeine dose rather than situational variables BRIEF.
  • DESIGN An independent measures design will be used, with each participant randomly allocated to one of the three caffeine conditions DETAIL; this avoids order effects (such as practice on the reaction-time task) that could otherwise inflate the apparent caffeine effect BRIEF.
Procedural steps (replicable)
  1. Participants arrive at the sports lab at 10 a.m. having abstained from caffeine for 12 hours, and are randomly allocated to one of three groups using a random number generator.
  2. Each participant is given a capsule (placebo, 100 mg or 200 mg caffeine) and waits 30 minutes for absorption.
  3. Participants complete 20 trials of the computerised reaction-time task; mean response time in milliseconds is recorded automatically.
  4. Mean reaction times are compared across the three conditions to test whether higher caffeine doses produce faster reaction times.
HarderBackground noise and exam performance (field experiment)
Scenario. Mei is a teacher in a secondary school who suspects that classroom noise affects student performance on short test papers. She has permission to vary the listening conditions during regular weekly vocabulary tests in three Year 10 classes. Plan a field experiment to investigate whether background noise affects test performance. Do not describe sample, sampling technique or ethical guidelines.
Sample answer (10 marks)
  • IV The independent variable will be the type of background noise during the test, with three levels: silence, low-volume classical music (50 dB), and recorded café-style chatter (50 dB) DETAIL; this will let Mei see whether noise type, rather than just noise volume, affects how Year 10 students perform on their vocabulary tests BRIEF.
  • DV The dependent variable will be vocabulary test score, operationalised as the number of correct answers out of 20 on a standard 10-minute weekly test DETAIL, giving Mei a precise quantitative measure of test performance under each noise condition BRIEF.
  • CONTROLS Controls will include: all three Year 10 classes use the same vocabulary test paper of equivalent difficulty, the test is held at the same time on the same day of the week, the noise volume is set to 50 dB using a calibrated speaker, and identical instructions are read from a script DETAIL. This ensures any difference in scores is due to the noise type rather than to test difficulty or time of day BRIEF.
  • DESIGN A repeated measures design will be used, with all three classes experiencing all three noise conditions across three consecutive weeks, counterbalanced so that the order of conditions differs between classes (Latin square: ABC, BCA, CAB) DETAIL. This removes participant variables — since the same students are tested under each condition — while counterbalancing controls order effects such as practice on weekly tests BRIEF.
  • Bonus location feature for field: the experiment is conducted in the students' usual classroom, which preserves the ecological validity of the test situation while still allowing Mei to manipulate the noise variable.
Procedural steps (replicable)
  1. The three Year 10 classes are assigned to one of the three counterbalancing orders (ABC, BCA, CAB).
  2. In Week 1, each class takes the weekly 20-item vocabulary test in their assigned condition (silence, classical music, or café chatter at 50 dB), with standardised instructions.
  3. This is repeated in Weeks 2 and 3 with the remaining two conditions, using equivalent vocabulary tests of comparable difficulty.
  4. Mean test scores for each student under each condition are compared to determine whether background noise type affects performance.

Technique 2 — Self-reports

EasierSleep habits in university students (questionnaire)
Scenario. Layla is a wellbeing officer at a university. She is concerned that students are not getting enough sleep. She wants to gather information from a large group of students about their sleep habits. Plan a questionnaire Layla could use. Do not describe sample, sampling technique or ethical guidelines.
Sample answer (10 marks)
  • QUESTION TYPE The questionnaire will use mainly closed questions to allow easy quantitative comparison across many students, with one open question at the end to capture rich qualitative data DETAIL; this combination lets Layla compare sleep patterns numerically across the student body while also understanding individual reasons in students' own words BRIEF.
  • EXAMPLES (4) Four sample questions: (1) "How many hours of sleep do you typically get on a weeknight? a) less than 4 b) 4–6 c) 6–8 d) more than 8"; (2) "How often do you use your phone in bed before sleeping? a) never b) sometimes c) often d) always"; (3) On a 5-point Likert scale: "I feel rested when I wake up — strongly disagree (1) to strongly agree (5)"; (4) Open question: "What, if anything, prevents you from getting enough sleep?"
  • SCORING Closed multiple-choice questions will be scored as the percentage of students choosing each option (e.g. % reporting fewer than 6 hours' sleep). Likert-scale items will be averaged across all respondents, where higher scores indicate greater feelings of rest DETAIL. Open-question responses will be analysed thematically, identifying common themes such as "academic stress" or "screen time" BRIEF, giving Layla actionable insight into student sleep at her university.
  • FORMAT The questionnaire will be delivered online, via a link sent to all students through their university email DETAIL. An online questionnaire is suited to this study because Layla wants to reach a large student population quickly and the format allows automatic data collation BRIEF.
Procedural steps (replicable)
  1. The 12-item online questionnaire is built (10 closed + 1 Likert + 1 open) and an anonymous response link generated.
  2. The link is emailed to all enrolled students, with a two-week response window.
  3. Closed responses are tabulated automatically; Likert means are calculated; open responses are coded thematically by two independent raters.
  4. Findings are summarised in a wellbeing report identifying patterns and barriers to adequate sleep.
HarderManagers' experience of burnout (semi-structured interview)
Scenario. Dr Patel is an organisational psychologist studying burnout among middle managers in tech companies. She wants to understand both how widespread it is and the specific situations that managers find most stressful. Plan an interview Dr Patel could conduct. Do not describe sample, sampling technique or ethical guidelines.
Sample answer (10 marks)
  • QUESTION TYPE The interview will combine closed questions (to give quantitative data on the prevalence of burnout symptoms) with open questions (to explore the situations managers find stressful in their own words) DETAIL. This dual format allows Dr Patel to both quantify burnout and understand the specific workplace contexts behind it BRIEF.
  • EXAMPLES (4) (1) Closed: "In the last month, how often have you felt emotionally exhausted by work? — never / rarely / sometimes / often / always"; (2) Closed Likert: "I feel I am making a positive difference at work — 1 (strongly disagree) to 5 (strongly agree)"; (3) Open: "Can you describe a recent situation at work where you felt overwhelmed?"; (4) Open follow-up: "What, if anything, helped you cope with that situation?"
  • INTERPRETATION Closed questions will be scored numerically (frequency counts, Likert means) DETAIL. Open responses will be transcribed verbatim and analysed using thematic analysis: two researchers will independently code transcripts to identify recurring themes (e.g. "deadline pressure", "lack of autonomy"), then compare coding to establish inter-rater reliability BRIEF. This gives Dr Patel both prevalence data and insight into the precise sources of burnout in tech middle management.
  • FORMAT A semi-structured interview will be used: Dr Patel will follow a fixed list of core questions, but is free to ask additional follow-up questions to clarify or probe interesting answers DETAIL. Semi-structured format is ideal here because it ensures consistency across managers (so prevalence figures are comparable) while still allowing the in-depth, contextual exploration that burnout research requires BRIEF.
Procedural steps (replicable)
  1. A core question schedule of 10 items (5 closed, 5 open) is finalised, plus a short list of follow-up prompts for the open items.
  2. Each interview is conducted face-to-face in a quiet meeting room and audio-recorded, lasting approximately 45 minutes.
  3. Recordings are transcribed verbatim; closed responses are tabulated and open responses are coded thematically by two independent raters who then agree on a final coding scheme.
  4. Quantitative scores and qualitative themes are integrated to produce a profile of burnout prevalence and its principal workplace triggers.

Technique 3 — Observational studies

EasierChildren's playground behaviour (naturalistic observation)
Scenario. Akello is researching how primary-school children behave during free play. She wants a precise record of which activities they choose and how often they interact with peers, in their normal playground environment. Plan an observational study Akello could conduct. Do not describe sample, sampling technique or ethical guidelines.
Sample answer (10 marks)
  • COVERT/OVERT The observation will be covert — Akello will watch from a window overlooking the playground rather than being visible to the children DETAIL. Covert observation is appropriate here because young children might alter their behaviour if they noticed an unfamiliar adult watching, reducing the validity of Akello's data on natural play BRIEF.
  • PARTICIPANT/NON-PARTICIPANT Akello will conduct a non-participant observation, recording behaviours from outside the play group rather than joining in DETAIL; this keeps her observations objective and avoids influencing the children's free-play behaviour, which is exactly what she wants to study BRIEF.
  • NATURALISTIC/CONTROLLED The study will be naturalistic — conducted in the children's usual school playground during a normal mid-morning break, with no manipulation of the environment DETAIL. This gives high ecological validity, since Akello wants to know how children actually play in real conditions, not how they behave in an artificial setting BRIEF.
  • STRUCTURED/UNSTRUCTURED A structured observation will be used, with a pre-defined behavioural checklist and event sampling DETAIL. Categories will include "playing alone", "playing in a group of 2–3", "playing in a group of 4+", "running game", "imaginative game", and "sitting/standing still", with a tally each time a behaviour occurs BRIEF. The checklist gives Akello clean quantitative data on what children actually do during free play.
Procedural steps (replicable)
  1. Akello positions herself at a window overlooking the playground 5 minutes before the morning break.
  2. Using event sampling, she tallies each occurrence of the six pre-defined behaviours during a 20-minute observation window.
  3. The procedure is repeated on five separate days to build a stable behavioural profile.
  4. Frequencies for each category are summed and compared to identify the most common activities and group sizes during free play.
HarderCafé customer queueing behaviour (participant + structured observation)
Scenario. Diego works in a busy city-centre café. His manager wants to know how customers behave when the queue gets long — whether they are patient, leave without ordering, or complain. Diego has agreed to combine working as a server with collecting data. Plan an observational study Diego could conduct. Do not describe sample, sampling technique or ethical guidelines.
Sample answer (10 marks)
  • COVERT/OVERT The observation will be covert — customers will not be told they are being observed for a study, since Diego is appearing to them as a normal café server DETAIL. Covert observation is appropriate because if customers knew they were being watched, they may behave more politely than usual, masking the real queueing behaviour the manager wants to understand BRIEF.
  • PARTICIPANT/NON-PARTICIPANT Diego will conduct a participant observation — he will continue working as a server while recording behaviour, embedded within the café environment DETAIL. This is essential here because his manager has asked him to use his existing role; it also gives him a unique vantage point to observe both the front and back of the queue BRIEF.
  • NATURALISTIC/CONTROLLED The setting is naturalistic — Diego will observe customers in the actual café during normal trading hours, with no changes to the environment, prices or service DETAIL; this gives the manager high ecological validity, since the data reflect customer behaviour in genuinely busy conditions rather than a contrived setup BRIEF.
  • STRUCTURED/UNSTRUCTURED A structured observation with a behavioural checklist and time sampling will be used. Diego will tally behaviours every 5 minutes during the lunch rush DETAIL. Categories will include: "customer waits patiently", "customer checks watch/phone", "customer sighs or rolls eyes", "customer complains verbally", and "customer leaves the queue without ordering" BRIEF. The structured checklist with time sampling gives the manager objective frequency data that can be linked to queue length.
Procedural steps (replicable)
  1. Diego prepares a tally sheet with the five behavioural categories and a column for queue length, kept beside the till.
  2. During the 12 p.m.–2 p.m. lunch rush, every 5 minutes he records (a) the current queue length and (b) the number of each category of behaviour observed in the previous 5 minutes.
  3. This is repeated on five working days to build a representative dataset.
  4. Behaviour frequencies are cross-tabulated with queue length to identify the threshold at which patience deteriorates and customers walk away.

Technique 4 — Correlational studies

EasierScreen time and sleep quality (correlation)
Scenario. Bashar is a sleep researcher who suspects that the more time teenagers spend on screens before bed, the worse they sleep. He cannot ethically force teenagers to use more or less screen time, so he plans to measure their existing habits. Plan a correlational study Bashar could conduct. Do not describe sample, sampling technique or ethical guidelines.
Sample answer (10 marks)
  • CO-VARIABLES The two co-variables will be (1) the amount of evening screen time and (2) self-reported sleep quality DETAIL. Both are measured rather than manipulated, which suits Bashar's situation since he cannot ethically alter teenagers' actual screen habits BRIEF.
  • CO-VARIABLE 1 Evening screen time will be operationalised as the average number of minutes spent on a phone or tablet between 8 p.m. and bedtime, recorded by the device's built-in screen-time tracker over a 7-day period and averaged DETAIL. This gives Bashar an objective measure of each teenager's actual evening screen exposure BRIEF.
  • CO-VARIABLE 2 Sleep quality will be operationalised as a teenager's score on the 19-item Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index, completed at the end of the 7-day period DETAIL; PSQI scores range from 0 to 21, with higher scores indicating worse sleep quality, giving Bashar a standardised, validated measure BRIEF.
  • GRAPH + DIRECTIONAL HYPOTHESIS A scatter graph will be plotted with mean evening screen time (minutes) on the x-axis and PSQI score on the y-axis, with each dot representing one teenager DETAIL. Bashar's directional (one-tailed) hypothesis is: "There will be a positive correlation between evening screen time and PSQI scores, such that teenagers with more evening screen time will have higher (worse) PSQI scores." A positive correlation would appear on the scatter graph as points trending from the bottom-left to the top-right BRIEF.
Procedural steps (replicable)
  1. Each teenager's phone/tablet screen-time tracker is set to log minutes between 8 p.m. and 12 a.m. for 7 consecutive days.
  2. At the end of the 7 days, each teenager completes the standardised PSQI questionnaire under quiet conditions.
  3. Each participant's mean evening screen time and total PSQI score are paired into a single dataset.
  4. The two co-variables are plotted on a scatter graph and examined for direction and strength of correlation.
HarderEmpathy and pro-social behaviour (correlation)
Scenario. Dr Romero is a social psychologist who wants to investigate whether people who score higher on empathy also help strangers more often. She will measure adult volunteers on both a personality test and a behavioural task. Plan a correlational study Dr Romero could conduct. Do not describe sample, sampling technique or ethical guidelines.
Sample answer (10 marks)
  • CO-VARIABLES The two co-variables will be (1) self-reported empathy and (2) helping behaviour towards strangers in a structured task DETAIL. Neither variable is manipulated — both are measured for each participant — which makes a correlation appropriate, especially because empathy is a stable trait that cannot ethically be manipulated BRIEF.
  • CO-VARIABLE 1 Empathy will be operationalised as a participant's total score on the 28-item Interpersonal Reactivity Index (IRI), with each item rated on a 5-point Likert scale, giving a final score ranging from 0 to 112 DETAIL. The IRI is a standardised psychometric measure of trait empathy, giving Dr Romero a reliable comparison across participants BRIEF.
  • CO-VARIABLE 2 Helping behaviour will be operationalised as the number of times (out of five staged opportunities) a participant offers help to a confederate posing as a stranger in need (e.g. dropping papers, asking for directions, requesting to use a phone) within a 30-minute walk through a city centre DETAIL; scores range from 0 to 5, giving Dr Romero a precise behavioural index of stranger-directed helping BRIEF.
  • GRAPH + DIRECTIONAL HYPOTHESIS A scatter graph will be plotted with IRI score (0–112) on the x-axis and number of helping acts (0–5) on the y-axis, each dot representing one participant DETAIL. Dr Romero's directional (one-tailed) hypothesis is: "There will be a positive correlation between IRI scores and the number of helping acts, such that participants with higher empathy scores will help on more occasions." A positive correlation would appear on the scatter as points trending from bottom-left to top-right BRIEF.
Procedural steps (replicable)
  1. Each participant individually completes the 28-item IRI in a quiet office and their total score is recorded.
  2. The participant is then asked to walk a fixed 30-minute route through the city centre with a researcher, supposedly to "discuss city navigation".
  3. Along the route, five confederates approach the participant at pre-arranged points, each presenting a brief opportunity to help; whether the participant helps (yes/no) is recorded discreetly by the researcher.
  4. Each participant's IRI score is paired with their helping score (0–5), and the pairs are plotted on a scatter graph to assess direction and strength of correlation.
V

Ethical guidelines

Two sets you must know — for human participants (BPS Code of Human Research Ethics, 2014) and for animals (BPS guidelines, 2012).

Human participants

The 9990 syllabus lists seven guidelines you should be able to describe, evaluate against real studies, and apply to novel scenarios.

1
Minimising harm

Participants should not face physical or psychological risk greater than they would meet in everyday life. Researchers should also maximise benefits to participants and wider society.

2
Valid (informed) consent

Participants must know enough about the study — its aim, procedure, and any risks — to make a genuine decision about whether to take part. Parental consent is needed for participants under 16.

3
Right to withdraw

Participants must know they can leave the study at any point — and remove their data afterwards — without penalty.

4
Lack of deception

Participants should not be deliberately misled about the aim or procedure. Where some deception is unavoidable, it must be minimal and the participants must be fully debriefed.

5
Confidentiality

Personal data and individual results must be kept securely and not shared outside the research team. Real names are usually replaced with codes or initials.

6
Privacy

The participant's personal physical and psychological space should not be invaded. Observations in private settings, for instance, are normally not acceptable.

7
Debriefing

At the end of the study, participants receive a full explanation of the aims and any deception used, and leave in the same psychological state in which they arrived.

Animal participants

For animal research, the 9990 syllabus expects you to know the following guidelines (based on the BPS guidelines for psychologists working with animals, 2012).

1
Minimising harm

Researchers must minimise harm to animals while maximising the benefits that flow from the research. A cost–benefit analysis is required.

2
Replacement

Wherever possible, animal experiments should be replaced with non-animal alternatives such as videos, simulations, or studies of human participants.

3
Species & strain

Researchers should choose a species and strain that is scientifically and ethically appropriate, and consider the animal's previous experience and welfare needs.

4
Numbers

Use the smallest number of animals consistent with valid results — calculated from pilot studies and statistical analysis, in line with the Animal Scientific Procedures Act (1986).

5
Procedures: pain & distress

Procedures likely to cause pain, suffering or distress must be carefully justified, performed under anaesthesia where applicable, and any adverse effects assessed and acted on immediately.

6
Procedures: housing

Caging must mirror important features of the natural environment and respect the social behaviour of the species. Isolation and crowding should be avoided.

7
Procedures: reward, deprivation & aversive stimuli

The animal's natural eating and drinking habits should be respected; deprivation and aversive stimuli should be no greater than is necessary to achieve the research aims, and only used when no alternative motivator exists.

Linking to studies Examiners often ask you to apply ethics to a named core study. Useful pairings: Milgram (deception, protection from harm); Bandura (children, lack of consent, exposure to aggression); Hassett (animal research — housing, species choice); Piliavin (covert field experiment — no consent or debrief).
VI

AS-level Issues & Debates

For each core study you should be able to discuss how it relates to the five issues and debates listed in the 9990 syllabus.

These five debates are explicitly named in the syllabus (page 15) and the Hodder Study and Revision Guide. Examiners reward answers that pick a clear position, support it with evidence from a study, and acknowledge a counter-point.

1. The application of psychology to everyday life

The practical use of a theory or research findings to improve processes or people's lives — for example, in physical or mental health, education, safety, or workplace productivity.

How to argue it: identify a real-world implication of the study's findings. Useful linking studies: Andrade's doodling research informs classroom note-taking; Pozzulo et al. on line-ups informs police identification procedures; Saavedra and Silverman on button phobia informs phobia treatment.

Tension: studies that produce highly applicable findings are often field-based, which can compromise control; studies high in control are often laboratory-based, which can compromise ecological validity. Real-world application requires both.

2. Individual and situational explanations

The extent to which a person's behaviour is controlled by factors unique to them (personality, physiology — individual) or by factors in the situation (the people, place, or social pressures present — situational).

How to argue it: identify whether the study explains behaviour using internal traits or external context. Milgram's obedience study is a classic situational explanation — ordinary people obeyed because of the authority figure and setting, not because of a "cruel personality".

Tension: behaviour is usually a product of both. Even Milgram's study shows individual variation (some participants refused), and even strongly individual explanations (like phobias) involve situational triggers.

3. Nature versus nurture

The extent to which behaviour, feelings or thinking results from innate genetic factors (nature) versus environmental influences such as learning and other people (nurture).

How to argue it: identify whether the study attributes behaviour to biology or to environmental experience. Hassett et al.'s monkey toy preferences study supports a nature explanation for sex-typed play; Bandura's Bobo doll study supports a nurture explanation for aggression.

Tension: the two interact. Even strongly biological behaviours unfold through experience, and even strongly learned behaviours are constrained by biology. Saying "purely nature" can also be socially sensitive when applied to human group differences.

4. The use of children in psychological research

The ethical and practical considerations involved when participants are under 16 — most importantly, the need for parental consent, age-appropriate language, and a thorough risk assessment.

How to argue it: children give us insights we cannot get from adults (e.g. Bandura on social learning, Pozzulo on eyewitness memory, Saavedra and Silverman on phobia) — but children also cannot give fully informed consent themselves and may be more vulnerable to harm.

Tension: excluding children from research means we cannot understand or improve children's lives; including them means accepting heightened ethical responsibility.

5. The use of animals in psychological research

The ethical and scientific considerations involved when research uses non-human animals — covered in detail by the BPS animal-research guidelines (replacement, species, numbers, procedures).

How to argue it: animal studies (e.g. Hassett et al. on monkeys, Fagen et al. on elephants) can answer questions we cannot ethically ask of humans, can use simpler nervous systems to model basic processes, and can use longitudinal designs across an animal's lifespan. But generalising from animals to humans is uncertain, and animals cannot give consent.

Tension: the more biologically similar the animal (e.g. primates), the better the generalisation — but also the greater the ethical concern. The BPS 3Rs (Replacement, Reduction, Refinement) are central.

Structure for a debate question A clean structure: (1) define the debate in your own words; (2) name the side the study sits on, with evidence; (3) acknowledge a counter-argument or interaction with the other side; (4) reach a brief conclusion that does not over-claim.
